Introduction to TMS-fNIRS

TMS-fNIRS represents an innovative integration of two powerful neuroscience technologies: Transcranial Magnetic Stimulation (TMS) and Functional Near-Infrared Spectroscopy (fNIRS). This combined methodology has emerged as a significant advancement in neuroimaging and neuromodulation, offering researchers and clinicians unique insights into brain function, connectivity, and response to stimulation.

This combined approach is vital as it allows for real-time causal inferences about brain function, directly observing how stimulating one area impacts local and networked brain activity Chang et al., 2025. Such insights are crucial for developing targeted therapies and deepening our understanding of neural circuits.

Basic Science and Principles

Transcranial Magnetic Stimulation (TMS)

Transcranial Magnetic Stimulation is a noninvasive neurostimulation technique that uses magnetic fields to influence brain activity. The procedure involves:

  • Using a magnetic coil held over the head to generate magnetic pulses
  • These pulses penetrate the skull and create electrical currents in specific brain regions
  • The electrical currents stimulate nerve cells, modulating brain function

TMS works on the principle of electromagnetic induction, where rapidly changing magnetic fields induce electrical currents in conductive tissues like the brain. This allows for targeted stimulation of specific brain regions without invasive procedures.

The primary clinical application of TMS has been in treating major depression, where it is FDA-approved. The technology aims to improve symptoms by stimulating specific areas of the brain associated with mood regulation, adhering to established consensus safety limits Rossini et al., 2024 (placeholder).

Functional Near-Infrared Spectroscopy (fNIRS)

Functional Near-Infrared Spectroscopy is a non-invasive neuroimaging technique that measures brain activity by:

  • Using near-infrared light to estimate cortical hemodynamic activity
  • Detecting changes in blood oxygenation and cerebral blood flow
  • Monitoring brain function through light propagation through the scalp and brain

fNIRS operates on the principle that near-infrared light can penetrate the skull and be absorbed differently by oxygenated and deoxygenated hemoglobin. By measuring these absorption patterns, fNIRS can track changes in hemoglobin concentration and oxygenation, providing real-time monitoring of brain activity.

The technology uses a set of near-infrared light sources and sensors (detectors) organized in an array called a montage. This setup allows for portable, real-time brain activity monitoring suitable for various research and clinical settings, though it is important to consider limitations such as optical penetration depth of approximately 1.5 cm and potential confounds from scalp blood flow Fantini et al., 2024 (placeholder).

Combined TMS-fNIRS Technology

The integration of TMS and fNIRS creates a powerful tool for neuroscience research and clinical applications. Key aspects of this combined technology include:

  • Simultaneous brain stimulation (via TMS) and monitoring of hemodynamic responses (via fNIRS)
  • Ability to observe immediate brain responses to targeted stimulation
  • Comprehensive assessment of both electrical brain activity and hemodynamic responses

The combined methodology offers several advantages:

  • Enables precise measurement of brain responses to stimulation
  • Provides more comprehensive brain function assessment
  • Allows for flexible positioning of TMS coils and optical sensors
  • Supports precise diagnosis, assessment, and targeted therapeutic interventions

TMS-fNIRS can be implemented in simultaneous mode, where fNIRS records during TMS, or interleaved mode, where fNIRS recording occurs in gaps between TMS pulse trains to minimize artifacts Addison et al., 2025 (placeholder).

Clinical Applications

TMS-fNIRS technology has demonstrated promising applications in various clinical fields, offering objective biomarkers and personalized treatment strategies.

Psychiatry

Major Depressive Disorder & Bipolar Disorder

  • TMS is FDA-approved for treatment-resistant depression; fNIRS may personalize targeting and assess response Chang et al., 2025.
  • fNIRS can guide TMS in bipolar depression, potentially optimizing treatment protocols Chang et al., 2024.

Other Psychiatric Conditions

  • Research is exploring TMS-fNIRS for anxiety disorders, providing real-time feedback on brain responses to stimulation Placeholder et al., 2024.
  • The combined approach is studied for diagnosing and monitoring schizophrenia, offering objective brain activity measures Placeholder et al., 2024.

Neurology

Stroke Rehabilitation

  • TMS-fNIRS may guide interventions to improve motor function post-stroke, personalizing therapy Rösch et al., 2024.
  • Theta Burst Stimulation guided by fNIRS is explored for enhancing recovery and neuroplasticity Placeholder et al., 2024.

Pain Management & Disorders of Consciousness

  • TMS-fNIRS is investigated for developing personalized pain management strategies by observing brain responses Placeholder et al., 2024.
  • fNIRS helps evaluate TMS effects in patients with DOC, assessing brain function objectively Placeholder et al., 2024.

Diagnostic Applications

  • TMS-fNIRS offers potential to supplement clinical evaluations by providing objective brain measurements for diagnosis Placeholder et al., 2024.

Limitations and Challenges

Despite its promise, TMS-fNIRS technology faces several limitations and challenges:

Technical Limitations

Signal Interference

  • Potential interference between TMS and fNIRS signal acquisition
  • Need for advanced signal processing techniques

Spatial Resolution

  • Limited depth penetration of fNIRS (primarily cortical)
  • Challenges in targeting deep brain structures

Integration Complexities

  • Technical challenges in simultaneous data collection
  • Synchronization issues between the two technologies

Methodological Challenges

Standardization

  • Lack of standardized protocols for combined TMS-fNIRS
  • Variability in methodological approaches across studies

Data Interpretation

  • Complexity in interpreting combined electrical and hemodynamic data
  • Need for advanced analytical frameworks

Research Gaps

  • Ongoing need for improved hardware and software integration
  • Limited large-scale validation studies

Key challenges include mitigating motion artifacts and isolating cortical signals from scalp blood flow interference IFCN Guideline, 2024 (placeholder). These factors can complicate data interpretation and require sophisticated solutions.

Future Directions

The future of TMS-fNIRS technology holds exciting possibilities, particularly in refining personalized treatments and deepening our understanding of brain dynamics.

Closed-Loop Adaptive TMS-fNIRS

Closed-loop systems represent a significant leap forward, where fNIRS data dynamically adjusts TMS parameters in real-time. This approach allows for brain-state dependent stimulation, potentially maximizing therapeutic efficacy by tailoring pulses to ongoing neural activity Opitz, 2025. Such systems could revolutionize treatment for various neurological and psychiatric conditions by enabling highly personalized and adaptive neuromodulation protocols Rösch et al., 2024.
